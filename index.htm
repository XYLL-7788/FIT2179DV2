Australia on the Move

A state-by-state story of where Australians travel, how long they stay, and where domestic tourism generates the most value.

1. The big picture

The first two charts show the overall scale of domestic overnight tourism. Large eastern states dominate total trip volume and total expenditure, but these totals do not tell the whole story.

Overnight domestic tourism is unevenly distributed across Australia. This chart compares the number of overnight trips received by each state and territory in December Quarter 2025.

Expenditure shows the economic side of domestic tourism. While New South Wales receives the largest number of overnight trips, spending also highlights the strong tourism role of Queensland and Victoria.

2. Looking beyond total numbers

Total trips and total spending favour larger states. To compare destinations more fairly, the next charts use derived measures: average spend per trip and spending per visitor night.

Total spending can be misleading because larger states naturally receive more trips. Average spend per trip provides a fairer comparison of how much each overnight trip is worth.

Key insight: the Northern Territory receives fewer trips overall, but it has the highest average spend per overnight trip.

Average trip length alone does not show the economic value of each stay. This chart compares how much regional expenditure is generated per visitor night. It helps identify destinations where each night of travel contributes more strongly to the tourism economy.

3. High volume versus high value

The next visualisations compare tourism volume with tourism value. This separates destinations that attract many trips from destinations that generate higher spending per trip.

This bubble chart combines three measures at once: average nights, average spend per trip, and total trip volume. The dashed lines show the average values across states and territories. Orange highlights destinations that are above average for both stay length and spending.

This chart compares each state’s trip volume rank with its average spend rank. Positive values show destinations where value rank is stronger than volume rank, while negative values show destinations that rely more on trip volume.

4. Comparing tourism roles

These non-map visualisations summarise the different roles that states and territories play in domestic tourism. They use derived rankings and tourism categories rather than only raw totals.

This heatmap compares how each state ranks across four tourism measures. It shows that a state can rank highly in total trips but lower in average spending value.

This custom quadrant chart summarises the previous findings. Instead of showing one raw number, it classifies each state by whether it is high or lower in trip volume and tourism value.

5. Tourism on the map

The maps add a geographic view to the story. Raw trip totals highlight where domestic travel is concentrated, while population-adjusted spending shows a different pattern of tourism intensity.

This map shows where domestic overnight trips are concentrated across Australia. Darker states received more overnight trips in December Quarter 2025.

Total expenditure favours larger states, so this map adjusts spending by population. It shows where tourism spending is stronger relative to the number of residents.

Data sources: Tourism Research Australia, Domestic Tourism Statistics, December Quarter 2025; Australian Bureau of Statistics, National, state and territory population, September 2025. Map boundary source: simplified Australian states GeoJSON. Derived measures include average spend per trip, spending per visitor night, trips per 1,000 residents, spending per resident, and rank-based tourism categories.
